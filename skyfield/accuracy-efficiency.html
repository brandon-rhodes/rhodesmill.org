Polar Motion¬∂

It was discovered more than a century ago that the Earth‚Äôs crust has a slight freedom to wobble with respect to the Earth‚Äôs axis of rotation in space, because the continents and ocean basis are bound to the planet‚Äôs mass only through the fluid coupling of our planet‚Äôs viscous mantle. In Skyfield you can see the size of the effect by loading an official data file from the International Earth Rotation Service (IERS) and measuring the maximum excursions of the polar motion parameters ùë• and ùë¶:

from skyfield.api import load
from skyfield.data import iers

url = load.build_url('finals2000A.all')
with load.open(url) as f:
    finals_data = iers.parse_x_y_dut1_from_finals_all(f)

ts = load.timescale()
iers.install_polar_motion_table(ts, finals_data)

tt, x_arcseconds, y_arcseconds = ts.polar_motion_table
print('x:', max(abs(x_arcseconds)), 'arcseconds')
print('y:', max(abs(y_arcseconds)), 'arcseconds')

x: 0.32548 arcseconds
y: 0.596732 arcseconds

In what kinds of Skyfield calculations does the exact position of the Earth‚Äôs crust come into play?

  • Polar motion affects the position of an observer on the Earth‚Äôs surface.
  • Polar motion therefore also affects the relative position of a target with respect to an observer on the Earth‚Äôs surface.
  • Polar motion directly affects altazimuth coordinates, since the polar angles ùë• and ùë¶ tilt the zenith and local horizon against which altitude and azimuth are measured for a particular observer.
  • Finally, polar motion affects the position of any observation target that‚Äôs located on the Earth‚Äôs surface ‚Äî for example, if you are calculating the position of a ground station from the perspective of a space probe.

To have Skyfield apply polar motion when computing positions and coordinates, simply install the IERS tables on your timescale object as shown in the example code above. Polar motion will be used everywhere that it applies.
